  <context>
    <section name="fundamentals">
      Competitive analysis is the systematic process of identifying, evaluating, and understanding competitors in your market space. It provides critical insights into market dynamics, customer preferences, industry trends, and strategic opportunities. Effective competitive analysis goes beyond surface-level feature comparisons to understand business models, go-to-market strategies, customer experience, and underlying value propositions.

      The strategic value of competitive analysis includes:
      - Identifying market gaps and unmet customer needs
      - Understanding pricing strategies and market positioning
      - Benchmarking product capabilities and user experience
      - Anticipating competitive moves and industry shifts
      - Informing product roadmap and strategic planning
      - Reducing risk by learning from competitors' successes and failures
      - Differentiating your offering in meaningful ways
    </section>

    <section name="types_of_competition">
      <subsection name="direct_competitors">
        Companies offering similar products to the same target market with comparable value propositions. These are your primary competitive threats and the most critical to analyze deeply.
      </subsection>

      <subsection name="indirect_competitors">
        Companies solving the same problem with different solutions or approaches. Though less obvious, these can be significant threats as technology and market preferences evolve.
      </subsection>

      <subsection name="emerging_competitors">
        Startups and new market entrants that may not be significant today but show potential for disruption. Monitor funding, talent acquisition, and early customer traction.
      </subsection>

      <subsection name="substitute_solutions">
        Alternative ways customers solve their problem, including manual processes, legacy systems, or "good enough" solutions that compete for budget and attention.
      </subsection>
    </section>

    <section name="competitive_intelligence_sources">
      <primary_sources>
        - Competitor websites, product documentation, and public demos
        - Pricing pages, packaging, and promotional materials
        - Job postings (reveals strategic priorities and technology stack)
        - Financial reports and investor presentations (public companies)
        - Patent applications and research publications
        - Conference talks and thought leadership content
      </primary_sources>

      <secondary_sources>
        - Customer reviews and feedback on third-party sites
        - Industry analyst reports and market research
        - Social media sentiment and community engagement
        - Employee reviews on Glassdoor, LinkedIn, Blind
        - Developer forums and technical communities
        - Partner and integrations ecosystem
      </secondary_sources>

      <proprietary_sources>
        - Win/loss analysis from sales calls
        - Customer interview data and competitive mentions
        - Lost deal analysis and competitive displacements
        - Partner feedback and market intelligence
        - Competitive trials and hands-on evaluation
      </proprietary_sources>
    </section>
  </context>

  <instructions>
    <phase name="1_identification">
      <step order="1">
        Define your market boundaries clearly. What problem space are you analyzing? What customer segments are relevant? What geography? Without clear scope, analysis becomes unfocused.
      </step>

      <step order="2">
        Create an initial competitor list through brainstorming and market research. Start with obvious direct competitors, then expand to include indirect and emerging threats. Aim for 5-15 competitors to analyze deeply.
      </step>

      <step order="3">
        Categorize competitors by type, market share, and strategic relevance. Prioritize your analysis based on competitive threat, strategic importance, and information availability. Not all competitors deserve equal attention.
      </step>

      <step order="4">
        For each competitor, gather basic information: founding year, funding status, team size, target market, core product offering, pricing model, and key differentiators. This helps identify patterns and outliers.
      </step>
    </phase>

    <phase name="2_data_collection">
      <step order="1">
        Create a structured data collection framework. What information do you need for each competitor? Product features, pricing, positioning, customer experience, sales model, technical architecture, company health. Use templates for consistency.
      </step>

      <step order="2">
        Collect publicly available information systematically. Start with competitor websites, documentation, and marketing materials. Capture screenshots and archive content, as these materials may change.
      </step>

      <step order="3">
        Conduct hands-on evaluation when possible. Sign up for trials, attend demos, use free tiers, or purchase access if strategically important. Direct experience provides insights you can't get from documentation alone.
      </step>

      <step order="4">
        Gather customer intelligence. Read reviews, analyze social media sentiment, talk to customers who use competitive products, interview sales team about competitive mentions. Customer experience is as important as feature lists.
      </step>

      <step order="5">
        Monitor competitive signals over time. Track pricing changes, feature announcements, hiring trends, partnerships, marketing campaigns, executive communications. Patterns reveal strategic direction.
      </step>
    </phase>

    <phase name="3_analysis">
      <step order="1">
        Analyze product capabilities and feature sets. Create feature comparison matrices showing what each competitor offers. But go deeper: evaluate implementation quality, user experience, integration capabilities, and workflow design.
      </step>

      <step order="2">
        Examine business models and pricing strategies. How do competitors monetize? What's their packaging strategy? Are they freemium, usage-based, enterprise licensing? How does pricing scale with usage or features?
      </step>

      <step order="3">
        Evaluate market positioning and messaging. What problems do they claim to solve? What value propositions do they emphasize? Who are they targeting? How do they differentiate? Positioning reveals strategic choices.
      </step>

      <step order="4">
        Assess customer experience and go-to-market approach. What's their sales process? Self-serve or sales-driven? How do they onboard customers? What support do they provide? Customer journey can be a competitive advantage.
      </step>

      <step order="5">
        Analyze technical architecture and capabilities when relevant. What technology stack do they use? What are their integration options? How do they handle security, compliance, scalability? Technical constraints enable or limit strategic options.
      </step>

      <step order="6">
        Evaluate company health and trajectory. For startups: funding history, investor quality, hiring trends, revenue growth. For public companies: financial performance, growth rates, market sentiment. Financial strength influences competitive behavior.
      </step>
    </phase>

    <phase name="4_synthesis">
      <step order="1">
        Identify patterns and themes across competitors. What features are table stakes? What are common weaknesses? What alternative approaches are emerging? Where is the market converging or diverging?
      </step>

      <step order="2">
        Map the competitive landscape visually. Use positioning maps to show where competitors cluster. Create quadrant analyses based on key dimensions like completeness of vision vs. ability to execute.
      </step>

      <step order="3">
        Identify competitive gaps and opportunities. What needs aren't being met? What problems are poorly solved? What customer segments are underserved? Where can you differentiate meaningfully?
      </step>

      <step order="4">
        Assess competitive strengths and weaknesses objectively. What does each competitor do well? Where do they struggle? What are their strategic constraints? Understanding weaknesses reveals opportunities.
      </step>

      <step order="5">
        Forecast competitive evolution. Where is the market heading? What capabilities will become table stakes? What disruptive threats might emerge? How might competitors respond to your moves?
      </step>
    </phase>

    <phase name="5_strategy">
      <step order="1">
        Define your competitive positioning. How will you differentiate? What customer segments will you target? What problems will you solve better than anyone else? Positioning should be authentic, defensible, and valuable.
      </step>

      <step order="2">
        Develop response strategies for key competitors. How will you win against their strengths? How will you exploit their weaknesses? What messaging will resonate with customers comparing options?
      </step>

      <step order="3">
        Inform your product roadmap based on competitive gaps and market trends. But prioritize based on your unique value proposition, not just matching features. Feature chasing is a losing strategy.
      </step>

      <step order="4">
        Create competitive playbooks for your teams. Sales needs battle cards. Marketing needs positioning guides. Product needs feature intelligence. Customer success needs migration strategies.
      </step>

      <step order="5">
        Establish ongoing monitoring processes. Competitive analysis isn't one-time. Set up alerts, schedule regular reviews, create feedback loops from customer-facing teams. Markets evolve continuously.
      </step>
    </phase>
  </instructions>

  <rules>
    <rule priority="1">
      Maintain objectivity. Don't dismiss competitors or overestimate your capabilities. Honest analysis leads to better strategy. Acknowledge competitive strengths and your own weaknesses.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="2">
      Focus on insights, not information. Data collection is useless without analysis and synthesis. Don't just catalog features—understand implications, patterns, and strategic significance.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="3">
      Prioritize depth over breadth for key competitors. Deep understanding of 3-5 key competitors is more valuable than shallow analysis of 20. Adjust depth based on strategic relevance and threat level.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="4">
      Always consider the customer perspective. Features matter less than customer outcomes. How do competitive options help customers achieve their goals? What experiences do they deliver?
    </rule>

    <rule priority="5">
      Distinguish between table stakes and differentiators. Table stakes are requirements to compete—features you must have to be considered. Differentiators are what make customers choose you. Focus resources on differentiators.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="6">
      Update analysis regularly. Markets change quickly. Quarter-old intelligence may be obsolete. Establish a review cadence based on market dynamics and strategic importance.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="7">
      Respect ethical boundaries. Don't misrepresent yourself to gather information. Don't use stolen data or confidential information. Competitive intelligence is not corporate espionage.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="8">
      Share insights broadly across the organization. Competitive intelligence shouldn't be siloed. Sales, marketing, product, and leadership all need access to relevant insights for decision-making.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="9">
      Validate assumptions with real customer feedback. Your perception of competitive strengths may not match customer experience. Regularly talk to customers who evaluated or use competitive products.
    </rule>

    <rule priority="10">
      Use competitive analysis to inform strategy, not dictate it. Don't react to every competitive move. Stay focused on your unique value proposition and customer needs. React strategically, not reflexively.
    </rule>
  </rules>

  <best_practices>
    <practice name="feature_matrices">
      Create detailed feature comparison matrices for deep product analysis. But don't just check boxes—evaluate implementation quality, user experience, integration depth, and workflow fit. Use scoring or weighting to reflect feature importance to customers. Group features into categories (core, advanced, specialized) for clarity. Include release dates to show feature evolution and innovation pace.
    </practice>

    <practice name="swot_analysis">
      Conduct structured SWOT analysis for key competitors. Strengths: What do they do exceptionally well? What are their durable advantages? Weaknesses: Where do they struggle? What constraints limit them? Opportunities: What market trends favor them? What strategic options do they have? Threats: What challenges do they face? What could disrupt them? Be specific and evidence-based.
    </practice>

    <practice name="pricing_analysis">
      Analyze pricing models, not just prices. Understand packaging strategy, feature tiers, usage limits, and upsell paths. Calculate total cost of ownership for realistic usage scenarios. Identify pricing strategies (penetration, skimming, value-based, freemium). Map pricing to customer segments and use cases. Understand discounting practices and negotiation flexibility. Pricing reveals value proposition and target customers.
    </practice>

    <practice name="customer_experience_mapping">
      Map the end-to-end customer journey for competitive products. How do customers discover, evaluate, purchase, onboard, use, and renew? What are the pain points and delight moments at each stage? Customer experience is a critical differentiator, especially for product-led growth. Use mystery shopping, trial experiences, and customer interviews to build accurate journey maps.
    </practice>

    <practice name="positioning_maps">
      Create visual positioning maps to show competitive landscape. Choose meaningful axes (e.g., simplicity vs. power, horizontal vs. vertical, platform vs. point solution). Plot competitors based on analysis, identify clusters and white space. Positioning maps reveal strategic patterns and differentiation opportunities. Use multiple maps with different axes for comprehensive view.
    </practice>

    <practice name="win_loss_analysis">
      Systematically analyze competitive wins and losses. Why do customers choose competitors? Why do they choose you? What criteria matter most? What objections come up? Conduct interviews with customers who considered competitive options. Share insights with product, marketing, and sales. Win/loss analysis provides unfiltered customer perspective.
    </practice>

    <practice name="patent_and_ip_analysis">
      For technology-driven products, analyze patent portfolios and R&D investments. Patents reveal strategic focus and technical direction. R&D spending indicates commitment to innovation. Look for patent filings in emerging areas—these signal future product direction. IP analysis helps forecast competitive evolution and identify potential barriers.
    </practice>

    <practice name="talent_analysis">
      Monitor competitor hiring patterns and team composition. Who are they hiring? What skills are they prioritizing? What roles are they growing? Hiring reveals strategic priorities and technical direction. Key executive hires can signal strategic shifts. Team size and growth rate provide insight into company health and ambitions.
    </practice>

    <practice name="partnership_ecosystem">
      Analyze competitor partnership and integration ecosystems. Who do they partner with? What platforms do they integrate with? What marketplaces do they participate in? Ecosystems can create network effects and switching costs. Integration breadth and depth reveal architectural philosophy and market strategy.
    </practice>

    <practice name="thought_leadership_monitoring">
      Track competitor thought leadership and content strategy. What topics do they focus on? What trends do they emphasize? How do they frame the market? Thought leadership reveals strategic positioning and target customers. Monitor blogs, whitepapers, conference talks, social media, and analyst briefings.
    </practice>

    <practice name="analyst_reports">
      Leverage industry analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC) for market context. Analyst reports provide frameworks, vendor evaluations, and trend analysis. But critically evaluate methodology—analyst perspectives may not align with your customer reality. Use as one input among many, not gospel truth.
    </practice>

    <practice name="quarterly_business_reviews">
      For public competitors, analyze quarterly earnings reports and investor presentations. Financial performance reveals business health and strategic focus. Management commentary provides insight into priorities and market view. Listen to earnings calls for unscripted insights. Track metrics like growth rate, customer acquisition cost, churn, and margins.
    </practice>

    <practice name="competitive_intelligence_sharing">
      Create regular mechanisms to share competitive intelligence across teams. Weekly or monthly competitive newsletters, battle cards for sales, positioning guides for marketing, feature briefings for product. Make intelligence accessible and actionable. Create a central repository or dashboard for competitive information.
    </practice>
  </best_practices>

  <anti_patterns>
    <anti_pattern name="analysis_paralysis" severity="high">
      Getting stuck in endless research without moving to action. Competitive analysis should inform decisions, not delay them. Set clear scope and timelines. Accept that you'll never have complete information. Move to synthesis and strategy once you have sufficient insight. Better to be directionally right and moving than perfectly analyzed and stuck.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="feature_chasing" severity="high">
      Building features just because competitors have them. This leads to bloated products and diluted differentiation. Focus on your unique value proposition and customer needs. Not all competitive features are strategic imperatives. Evaluate whether features align with your positioning and serve your target customers. Sometimes the best response to a competitive feature is not to match it but to double down on your differentiation.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="confirmation_bias" severity="medium">
      Interpreting competitive intelligence to confirm existing beliefs. We tend to notice data that supports our views and ignore contradictory evidence. Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Challenge assumptions. Invite diverse perspectives in analysis. Use structured frameworks to reduce bias.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="competitive_mirroring" severity="high">
      Copying competitor strategy, positioning, or messaging rather than developing your own. Me-too strategies rarely win. Customers have no reason to switch if you're the same. Even if you're better, better is rarely sustainable. Differentiation comes from being different in ways that matter to customers.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="overindexing_on_public_competitors" severity="medium">
      Focusing only on visible, well-known competitors while ignoring emerging threats or indirect competition. Startups often disrupt markets before they're on anyone's radar. Indirect competitors can pivot into your space. Regularly scan for emerging competitors and alternative solutions.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="shallow_analysis" severity="medium">
      Surface-level feature comparisons without deeper analysis. Checking boxes tells you what competitors have, not how well it works or whether customers care. Go beyond features to understand implementation quality, user experience, business model, and strategic intent. Depth beats breadth for key competitors.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="static_analysis" severity="medium">
      Treating competitive analysis as a one-time project rather than ongoing process. Markets evolve quickly. Analysis becomes stale. Competitive intelligence that isn't regularly updated is worse than useless—it's misleading. Establish processes for continuous monitoring and periodic deep refreshes.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="ignoring_customer_reality" severity="high">
      Analyzing competitors through your lens rather than customer perspective. What you think matters may not be what customers care about. Features you dismiss may be crucial to certain segments. Always validate analysis with customer feedback. Talk to customers who use competitive products. Understand their actual experience, not your assumptions.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="overemphasis_on_shiny_features" severity="medium">
      Focusing on innovative or exciting features while neglecting table stakes and fundamentals. Advanced features get attention, but customers often choose based on reliability, ease of use, performance, support, and other fundamentals. Don't overlook the boring but essential capabilities that determine real-world success.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="siloed_intelligence" severity="medium">
      Keeping competitive intelligence locked in research or strategy teams without sharing broadly. Sales, marketing, product, and customer success all need competitive insights to be effective. Create mechanisms to distribute relevant intelligence to the right people at the right time. Intelligence sitting in a report provides no value.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="competitive_fear" severity="medium">
      Overreacting to competitive moves rather than staying strategic. Not every competitive feature requires a response. Not every market shift is a threat. Maintain strategic discipline. Focus on your unique value and customer needs. Respond strategically, not reflexively. Sometimes the best response to competitive noise is strategic silence.
    </anti_pattern>

    <anti_pattern name="neglecting_indirect_competition" severity="medium">
      Focusing only on direct competitors while ignoring substitute solutions and alternative approaches. Customers don't care about market boundaries—they care about solving problems. Manual processes, legacy systems, and general-purpose tools are often your biggest competition. Understand all the ways customers solve their problem today.
    </anti_pattern>
  </anti_patterns>

        - **Price Pressure:** SMB customers gravitating to affordable alternatives

        - Strengths in enterprise and ecosystem are durable advantages

    <error_scenario name="incomplete_information">
      <problem>
        Critical information about competitors is unavailable or outdated. Private companies, emerging competitors, or international markets may have limited public information.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Use triangulation from multiple indirect sources
        - Leverage customer and partner networks for insights
        - Make reasoned inferences based on available data
        - Clearly document assumptions and information gaps
        - Focus on available information while acknowledging limitations
        - Consider investing in market research reports for critical gaps
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>

    <error_scenario name="misleading_signals">
      <problem>
        Competitive announcements, marketing materials, or public statements may not reflect reality. Companies exaggerate capabilities, overstate traction, or send strategic signals.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Validate claims through hands-on evaluation when possible
        - Cross-reference claims with customer reviews and feedback
        - Look for evidence beyond marketing (technical docs, APIs, integrations)
        - Analyze hiring and investment patterns for real priorities
        - Be skeptical of vaporware and pre-announcements
        - Focus on shipped products and customer experiences
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>

    <error_scenario name="rapidly_changing_landscape">
      <problem>
        Market moves faster than analysis cycle. Competitors launch features, change pricing, or pivot strategy after research is complete. Analysis becomes outdated quickly.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Establish ongoing monitoring processes for key competitors
        - Set up alerts for significant competitive changes
        - Create living documents that can be updated quickly
        - Prioritize analysis of stable, strategic factors over tactical details
        - Regularly refresh analysis on a quarterly or semi-annual basis
        - Build intelligence sharing into regular team rituals
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>

    <error_scenario name="overwhelming_volume">
      <problem>
        Too many competitors or too much information leads to analysis paralysis. Difficulty prioritizing what matters and synthesizing insights.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Segment competitors by strategic importance and focus depth accordingly
        - Use structured frameworks to organize information consistently
        - Set clear scope and boundaries for analysis
        - Move from breadth to depth iteratively
        - Focus on patterns and themes rather than exhaustive detail
        - Create summary views for quick consumption
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>

    <error_scenario name="analyst_bias">
      <problem>
        Analyst's existing beliefs, preferences, or loyalties color interpretation. Confirmation bias leads to selective attention to data that supports pre-existing views.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Use structured frameworks to ensure comprehensive analysis
        - Actively seek disconfirming evidence and alternative perspectives
        - Involve multiple analysts with diverse viewpoints
        - Challenge assumptions and document reasoning
        - Validate conclusions with external data sources
        - Create devil's advocate reviews of key conclusions
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>

    <error_scenario name="data_quality_issues">
      <problem>
        Conflicting information from different sources, outdated data, or unreliable intelligence sources lead to inaccurate conclusions.
      </problem>

      <solutions>
        - Prioritize primary sources and direct evidence
        - Cross-reference multiple sources for validation
        - Date-stamp all information and track currency
        - Use confidence ratings for uncertain intelligence
        - Clearly distinguish between fact, inference, and speculation
        - Update analysis as new information becomes available
      </solutions>
    </error_scenario>
  </error_handling>
